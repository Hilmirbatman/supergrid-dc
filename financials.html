What these numbers cover

A full 500 MW data center costs roughly €5 billion to build (2025 benchmarks of about $11 million per MW). The figures on this page cover the power-delivery and cooling subsystem that superconductivity changes, around 1 % of the total build. Generators, UPS, building shell and racks are identical to a conventional facility, so they fall out of the comparison.

Capital cost

More to build first, competitive at scale.

As an unproven system, the first unit carries a first-of-a-kind development cost of about €25 M for engineering, certification, integration and contingency. That makes the first build more expensive than conventional. As the design is repeated across a fleet, development is amortized and the recurring subsystem cost falls below conventional, because the superconducting design removes a step-down transformer and shrinks the civil works.

The proven cable saving is physics: a superconductor carries current without resistive loss. The projected cooling efficiency is the larger figure, but it reflects a shared-loop design assumption that a conventional facility could also pursue. It is presented as upside, not as the basis for the business case.

Superconductor-specific value

What copper cannot deliver.

These streams come from the superconductor's form factor and behaviour, independent of the cooling assumption.

🏢 Density

Reclaimed floor space sold as white-space or avoided new-build, a Munich-style footprint gain indoors.

🛡️ Reliability

A fault-current-limiting section protects rectifiers and racks from grid transients.

⏱️ Grid timing

Higher power density per connection point helps secure 500 MW before transmission queues clear.

📡 Simplicity

PLC removes a parallel data cable, and one cryogenic system replaces cable cooling plus chillers.
