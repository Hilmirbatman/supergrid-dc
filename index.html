The problem

Power and heat are the wall AI is hitting.

Data center electricity demand is projected to climb from roughly 415 TWh in 2024 to about 945 TWh by 2030 (IEA, Energy & AI, 2025). Operators need to secure around 500 MW per campus before grid bottlenecks stall expansion, and new transmission lines take 4 to 8 years in advanced economies.

Conventional copper feeds become physically enormous at these currents, and the cooling system fights waste heat the cables themselves create. Today the two biggest constraints, getting power in and getting heat out, are solved separately. They should be one system.

🌡️ Why superconductors

A high-temperature superconductor (YBCO tape, cooled by liquid nitrogen) carries enormous current with zero resistive loss. At ±30 kV bipolar DC, 500 MW flows as just 8.33 kA per pole, half the unipolar current, with no AC losses at all.

The cable must stay below roughly 93 K, but that cold loop is an asset rather than pure overhead. The same liquid nitrogen that keeps the cable superconducting carries away rack heat. The cost center becomes shared infrastructure.

The technology

Three innovations, one cryogenic spine.

Power delivery, cooling and data integrated into a single superconducting system.

🔌 HTS power cable

A ±30 kV bipolar DC superconducting cable runs from grid entry to the electrical bays. Zero conductor loss, a fraction of the footprint, and it feeds the rack rectifiers directly, eliminating a step-down transformer.

❄️ Shared LN₂ cooling

The closed-loop liquid nitrogen that keeps the cable cold doubles as the cold side of the rack-cooling loop through a heat exchanger. One cryogenic system replaces cable cooling and separate chillers.

📡 Power-line data

Power-line communication (PLC) sends monitoring and control data over the HTS cable itself, removing a parallel data-cable run and simplifying the rack-to-grid path.

Industry expertise

Validated against commercial HTS practice.

NKT A/S, the Danish high-voltage cable manufacturer headquartered in Brøndby, is an active developer of commercial HTS cable systems. The concept has been reviewed with Olfert Holte of NKT to test its technical feasibility and economics.

“Supply us with a cable that can provide 500 MW at 30 kV. It feeds the rectifiers directly and lets you take a whole transformer out of the pipeline.” Olfert Holte, NKT

🏙️ The Munich precedent

Munich's SuperLink HTS project demonstrates the value: a single half-metre trench replacing five conventional cable systems. Inside a data hall, the equivalent gain is reclaimed floor space rather than kilometres of street.

♻️ Effectively no aging

The superconducting tape has no meaningful aging mechanism; failures are mechanical rather than gradual. The model evaluates the tape over a 40-year life, with only the cryogenic plant scheduled for mid-life replacement.

🎯 An open market

HTS inside a data center has not been deployed before. Superconducting economics require at least 50 % continuous loading, a threshold AI workloads comfortably exceed.

🧊

Material footprint

Rare-earth content is minimal, on the order of 1 kg of yttrium per 100 km of tape, so the system is not constrained by exotic-material supply at data-center scale.
