Under the hood

How it's calculated.

Every result above comes from the chain below. orange marks an adjustable input Every step is explicit, with no hidden assumptions.

0 The one-line idea

We build a full cost & benefit picture for the HTS concept and for a conventional 500 MW data center, then report the difference. A positive NPV means the HTS option creates more value than it costs, versus building the conventional one.

Result = (value & cost of HTS option) − (value & cost of conventional baseline)

1 Physics & sizing

The facility is first turned into physical quantities. The cable current is what makes superconductors interesting, and at bipolar DC it is half the unipolar value.

Annual energy = facility MW × 8760 h × load factor
Cable current = MW × 1000 ÷ (2 × voltage kV) // bipolar DC, per pole → kA
Conductor loss = loss % × MW // copper ≈ 0.8% · HTS = 0

2 Cryogenics, the cost of cold

Keeping the cable at 77 K isn't free. A refrigerator pays an energy penalty set by thermodynamics (Carnot), derated by real-world efficiency. This is computed, not guessed.

Carnot COP = 77 ÷ (293 − 77) = 0.357
Real COP = Carnot × 0.18 efficiency = 0.064
Liquefier power = cable heat load ÷ Real COP // small, the cable is well insulated

3 Cooling electricity

Cooling is the biggest energy item in any data center. The conventional case runs chillers; the Full Concept claims a better effective COP from the shared loop. This is the soft assumption the honest-split bar isolates.

Cooling power = annual energy × cooling share × (chiller COP ÷ shared COP)
// turn "Count cooling claim" OFF → shared COP = chiller COP → saving disappears

4 Build cost (CAPEX)

Bottom-up, not a lump sum. The HTS cable is priced by current × length; the conventional case carries a transformer the HTS design deletes; and raw-tech reality is the development line you toggle.

HTS cable = 2 × current(kA) × length × €/kA·m (tape)
CAPEX = cable + cryo plant + rectifier + switchgear + safety + civil + R&D
         + first-of-a-kind development // HTS only, if the switch is on
// conventional instead carries: MW × €30,000/MVA transformer

5 Running cost (OPEX) & benefits

Yearly costs, plus the value streams. The "defensible" HTS benefit is the eliminated cable loss; the density benefit is the floor space you free up.

OPEX = (cooling + liquefier + losses) MWh × €/MWh + MW×O&M + CAPEX×insurance
Density benefit = floor space m² × white-space €/m²·yr
Reliability = downtime min avoided × €/min + FCL avoided fault

6 Returns: NPV, payback, LCOE

We lay out 25 years of cashflows (escalating electricity, a liquefier replacement at year 15), discount them to today's money, and compare to the baseline.

NPV = Σ (HTS cashflow − baseline cashflow)year ÷ (1 + discount rate)year
Payback = first year cumulative difference turns positive
LCOE = lifetime cost (present value) ÷ lifetime MWh delivered // €/MWh

7 Why the "honest split" matters

The headline benefit splits into two very different things. We keep them visually separate so a soft assumption can never masquerade as proven superconductor value.

Defensible (HTS) = avoided cable loss // physics, high confidence
Soft (cooling) = cooling improvement // a conventional DC could chase it too

8 What "build cost" means here (scope boundary)

This is the single most misread number, so it's worth being explicit. The CAPEX above is not the cost of the whole data center. It is the power-delivery and cryogenic subsystem that superconductivity changes.

Full facility (2025 benchmarks) ≈ €/MW × MW // ~$11M/MW standard, $15 to $20M/MW AI
  → a 500 MW DC ≈ €5 billion+ all-in
Electrical share = 40 to 45% · Cooling share = 15 to 25% // of total build
// generators, UPS, PDUs, shell, racks are identical HTS vs conventional → they cancel

Sources: Cushman & Wakefield 2025 cost guide; DatacenterDynamics; iRecruit 2026 benchmarks; Turner & Townsend. Because the facility cost is the same on both sides, it sits in the model as context (the banner up top), not in the NPV.

Confidence of the inputs

Solid: facility power, voltage, cable current, operating temp, electricity price.  Soft (treat with care): HTS tape cost, shared-cooling COP, white-space value, development cost. The soft ones move the answer most, which is why they are adjustable rather than fixed.
